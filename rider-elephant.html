The Rider, the Elephant, and Procrastination

Jonathan Haidt's “rider and elephant” theory helps explain why people often know what they should do but still struggle to do it. Procrastination is not always laziness. Sometimes students delay work because the task feels too stressful, too big, or too unclear.

The rider may know that a student should start early, sleep earlier, or stop scrolling. However, the elephant may feel fear, stress, tiredness, or resistance. When a task feels overwhelming, avoiding it can give short-term relief, even though it creates more stress later.

Why Knowing Is Not Always Enough

A difficult task can create pressure before the work even begins. The rational part of the mind may understand that the task should be started, but the emotional part may want to avoid stress, failure, or discomfort.

This is why meaningful learning should not only tell students what they should do. It should also help them understand why they struggle to act. From a psychology perspective, behavior is shaped by emotion, pressure, habit, and environment.

Helping the Rider and Elephant Work Together

One way to reduce procrastination is to make the first step very small. Instead of trying to finish a whole assignment, students can start by opening the document, writing one sentence, or working for only five minutes.

This small-step method reduces the pressure on the elephant. Once students begin, it often becomes easier to continue. This approach focuses on action rather than perfection, and it turns theory into something students can actually use in daily life.
